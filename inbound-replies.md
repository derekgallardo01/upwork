# Upwork inbound reply templates

Tested-pattern responses to the messages you'll actually get on Upwork.
Pair with [`upwork-playbook.md`](upwork-playbook.md) for the scan + bid
process and [`profile-and-proposals.md`](profile-and-proposals.md) for
the outbound side.

Last revised 2026-06-28.

---

## How to use this file

1. Find the closest matching scenario.
2. Customise the bracketed `<…>` parts (5-30 seconds).
3. Send.

**Do not** send any of these unmodified — Upwork clients sniff a copy-
paste and downgrade you. The skeleton is the wins, the personalisation
is the conversion.

---

## A. After they replied to your proposal

### A1. "What's your hourly rate?" (rate anchor needed)

> Hi <name>,
>
> Thanks for replying. Rate-wise I run two bands:
>
> - **Fixed-price** for scoped builds (most engagements) — the
>   <$X,XXX> in my proposal was for the full scope as I read it.
> - **Hourly** for ongoing / undefined work — **<$110-150/hr USD>**
>   depending on the engagement.
>
> If you'd like to firm up the scope so we can stay fixed-price, I can
> do a paid 1-hour scoping call (<$150>, credited against the fixed
> price if we proceed). That usually surfaces the unknowns before they
> become invoices.
>
> Happy to send the SOW structure I'd use — want me to send it across?
>
> — Derek

**Why it works:** anchors the *real* rate, offers a fixed-price escape
hatch, ends with a one-click yes/no follow-up.

### A2. "Can you start today / this week?"

> Hi <name>,
>
> Yes — I can start <this week / Monday>. Two things to set up first
> that will save us a week's worth of rework:
>
> 1. A 1-hour paid scoping call (<$150>) to confirm <data shape / scope
>    boundaries / access requirements> — I want to make sure the SOW I
>    quote is the one we actually build.
> 2. <Sandbox tenant access / sample data / access to the relevant
>    SharePoint site>.
>
> If we can get those today I can start the build tomorrow. Want me to
> send a calendar link?
>
> — Derek

**Why it works:** doesn't push back on urgency (which a real client
hates), but routes it through a scoping call that protects you.

### A3. "We don't have budget for $X — what's the lowest you could do?"

> Hi <name>,
>
> Fair question. Two ways to land closer to your number:
>
> **Option A — same price, narrower scope.** I'd drop <specific
> deliverable, e.g. "the handover Loom video" or "the second eval
> corpus" or "the production cutover">. We'd still ship a working
> <thing>; you'd just inherit a smaller version of <what got dropped>.
>
> **Option B — staged engagement.** Phase 1 (<$smaller>): <minimum
> viable deliverable>. Phase 2 (separately quoted later, only if you
> want it): <the remainder>. This caps your risk if Phase 1 doesn't
> deliver the result you hoped.
>
> What I won't do is hold the price and quietly cut corners on the
> build quality — you'll feel that 6 months in.
>
> Which option fits better?
>
> — Derek

**Why it works:** offers two concrete counter-options, names what you
won't do (signals integrity), ends with a forced choice.

### A4. "Send us a free sample / do a small test project to prove yourself"

> Hi <name>,
>
> I don't do unpaid samples — too many clients have asked, finished the
> "test", and never come back. What I do offer is a **paid 1-hour
> scoping call (<$150>)** that's credited against the fixed price if
> we proceed.
>
> In that hour I'll: <look at your data / review your existing flow /
> evaluate the doc set>, give you a written recommendation, and price
> the build honestly — including telling you if a no-code tool already
> does the job.
>
> If you're not comfortable with that, I'd suggest looking at the
> runnable demos in my profile — they're real working code you can
> clone right now:
>
> - <Most relevant repo link>
> - <Second most relevant>
>
> Want to book the scoping call?
>
> — Derek

**Why it works:** firm "no" on free work, replaces it with a low-risk
paid alternative, gives them a way to evaluate you without paying.

### A5. "Can you just join our team's standup tomorrow / hop on a quick call?"

> Hi <name>,
>
> Happy to. Two options:
>
> - **Free 15-minute intro call** if it's "are we a fit + what's your
>   process" — I'll send a calendar link.
> - **Paid 1-hour scoping call (<$150>)** if it's "let's look at the
>   actual problem and figure out scope" — that one delivers a written
>   recommendation + price afterward.
>
> Either way I'll come prepared with questions specific to your post.
> Which is the better fit?
>
> — Derek

**Why it works:** doesn't refuse the call (which seems rude), but
forces them to pick the paid track if it's actually working time.

### A6. Silence after a long thread — the "are we still on?" nudge

*(Send 5-7 days after your last message that wasn't replied to.)*

> Hi <name>,
>
> Looping back on this — happy to grab a slot this week if it's still
> live, or close the loop politely if priorities shifted on your end.
> Either's fine; I just don't want to keep refreshing my inbox.
>
> — Derek

**Why it works:** zero pressure, gives them a graceful exit, signals
you're not desperate. About 1 in 4 of these convert.

## B. Saying no — gracefully but firmly

### B1. "Job posting is below your floor"

> Hi <name>,
>
> Thanks for considering me. This one's below the floor I work at right
> now (<$X/hr> for AI / Power Platform builds), so I'll pass — but I
> didn't want to leave you hanging.
>
> For the budget you've got, I'd suggest <one or two specific
> alternatives — Upwork's "Verified" filter, a junior dev for the
> simpler parts, a no-code tool like Make.com if it fits>.
>
> Best of luck with the project.
>
> — Derek

**Why it works:** says no without judging, gives them genuine help on
the way out. They'll remember and might come back when the budget is
bigger.

### B2. "Job is outside your wheelhouse"

> Hi <name>,
>
> Thanks for reaching out. This one's just outside what I focus on — I
> stay in the Microsoft 365 / AI agent / Power Platform lane, and your
> project would benefit more from someone whose day job is <Salesforce
> / native iOS / data engineering>.
>
> A couple of categories that filter that well on Upwork: <"Salesforce
> Developer" / "iOS Developer" / "Data Engineering">. The folks with
> 50+ reviews in those tags will get you a better result than I would.
>
> Good luck.
>
> — Derek

**Why it works:** honest positioning, recommends the right tool for
their job (not yours), preserves your reputation.

### B3. "Red-flag job — sketchy, unclear, or wants free work"

> Hi <name>,
>
> Thanks for the message. I don't think we're the right fit for this
> one — wishing you the best with the project.
>
> — Derek

**Why it works:** doesn't explain, doesn't engage. Short replies to
red-flag clients save the most time.

### B4. "You're already booked"

> Hi <name>,
>
> Thanks for reaching out. I'm booked through <end of <month>>, so I
> can't start this one in your timeframe. If it's not urgent and
> you're happy to wait until <date>, I'd be keen — otherwise no hard
> feelings if you go with someone else.
>
> — Derek

**Why it works:** firm boundary, leaves the door open. Surprising
number of clients say "we'll wait" when they hear someone's already
booked — scarcity sells.

## C. After the engagement

### C1. Asking for a review (send 7-14 days after final delivery)

> Hi <name>,
>
> Hope the <thing> is settling in. Quick ask — would you be willing to
> leave a short review on the Upwork engagement? Even one or two lines
> about <specific outcome — e.g. "the time saved on the morning
> dispatch sync" / "the eval set catching the bad answer before it
> shipped"> would help me a lot.
>
> No pressure if not — I know you're busy.
>
> Also, if anything in the <flow / agent / dashboard> needs a tweak in
> the next couple of weeks, that's covered under the post-go-live
> support window — just shout.
>
> — Derek

**Why it works:** asks for the specific kind of review (mentions an
outcome they can describe), keeps the door open for the support window
naturally.

### C2. Polite "no" to a scope-creep request during the support window

> Hi <name>,
>
> Happy you want more — that one's outside what we scoped in the SOW
> §2, so it's a change request. Quick options:
>
> - <Option A>: <small scope, $X, can do this week>
> - <Option B>: <larger scope including <thing>, $Y, 2 weeks>
>
> Want me to send a short addendum for whichever fits?
>
> — Derek

**Why it works:** doesn't say "no" alone (which sours the
relationship); reframes as a forked choice they own. Most clients
pick Option A.

### C3. Engagement closing — keeping the door open

> Hi <name>,
>
> All wrapped — handover pack delivered, support window through
> <date>. It's been a genuine pleasure.
>
> If anything related comes up later (a second flow, a dashboard on
> top of the data, a Copilot agent over what we just built), I'd be
> happy to scope it. Repeat-client rate is <10-15%> off my standard
> price.
>
> — Derek

**Why it works:** signals you'd welcome the next engagement; the
discount is a small but real reason for them to come to you first.

## D. Inbound from invites (Upwork "Invited to interview")

### D1. Invite that's a good fit

*(Reply within 4 hours — invite-response speed massively affects
their perception.)*

> Hi <name>,
>
> Thanks for the invite — this one looks like a strong fit.
>
> <One sentence demonstrating you read the post.>
>
> I've built this exact pattern; my <relevant repo link> is the
> reference. Happy to do a paid 1-hour scoping call (<$150>, credited
> against the fixed price) to confirm scope and price the next step.
>
> Two questions to keep this short:
>
> 1. <one specific question that proves you read the post>
> 2. What does "done" look like 30 days after go-live?
>
> Want to send a calendar link?
>
> — Derek

### D2. Invite that's not quite right

> Hi <name>,
>
> Thanks for the invite. To be straight with you — <one-sentence
> mismatch, e.g. "this looks more like a Salesforce engagement than
> the Microsoft work I focus on">. I don't think I'm the right fit,
> but I appreciate you considering me.
>
> If something more in the M365 / Copilot / Power Platform space
> opens up later, I'd love to hear about it.
>
> — Derek

**Why it works:** honest no, leaves the door open, costs nothing.

## E. Cadence — when to follow up vs. when to walk

| State | Wait | Next move |
|-------|------|-----------|
| Sent proposal, no reply | 4 days | One nudge (template A6); stop after that |
| Replied to proposal, then silence | 5-7 days | One nudge (template A6); stop after that |
| Scoped, awaiting SOW signature | 3 days | "Want me to clarify anything before you sign?" |
| Invoiced, no payment | 7 days past due | Polite "any blockers on payment?" — escalate to Upwork support at 14 days |
| Engagement closed, no review | 14 days | One ask (template C1); never again |

The rule: **one nudge, then walk.** Hounding clients makes you look
desperate and costs more goodwill than the chance of conversion is
worth.

---

## What's NOT in here

- Anything that pushes a client to commit before they're ready.
- Anything that sounds like "I really need this work."
- Long apologetic openings.

The best Upwork replies are short, direct, and end with a clear
yes/no question. If a reply you're considering doesn't end with a
forced choice, it's probably too long.
